# The Neuroscience of Memory

When a memory is formed it creates a physical connection between two neurons. 

**Engram**: The physical trace of a memory preserved into the cortex. 

Every time a memory is activated the association gets stronger and the memory gets easier to retrieve. This is similar to weight training with the brain. 

Memories are associated with the hippocampus but are not stored there, instead it's where they are formed and re-consolidated.  
# Sensory Memory

Before information can be translated and stored, your brain needs to know what to actually store. What you make think is necessary to remember isn't always what your brain thinks is important to remember. 

**Sensory memory** takes in and stores sensory information in a relatively unaltered form for very brief periods of time. Researchers believe that sensory memory is what allows us to perceive the world as a unified whole rather than a series of staggered images and sounds. Sensory memory holds on to information in our nervous systems long enough for us to stitch one moment of our experience to the next.

**Sensory Memory** allows us to notice things but not process them. We are able to avoid someone if we need to and then get rid of the information since we don't need it anymore. 

# Working Memory

Working Memory, aka short-term memory, holds information at the front of your mind. For example, you use your working memory to remember the beginning of a sentence while reading the end of the sentence. 

## Representation 

The kind of information a memory system contains. Often this is described in terms of how the information is represented or coded. 

For example, when you are asked to count to 10 silently you probably think the numbers in a voice in your head. This is called the **inner voice**. It is worth noting that some people do not have this.

Similarly, there is an **inner eye** which allows people to visualize things in their head such as spinning an apple in their head. There are also people who do not have this as well, a condition called **aphantasia**.

## Duration 

**Duration** is how long you can keep something in your working memory before you forget it. 

Theoretically, **duration** can be infinite and you can hold onto something in your working memory, that is if you can continuously **rehearse** it .

**Rehearsal** is the process of repeating information to yourself helping you "re-hear" the information over and over to remember it better. 

## Capacity

**Capacity** is how much can be stored in your working memory at one time. 

George Miller suggested that the average person could hold 7 separate pieces of information at a time.

Most psychologists agree with this with the caveat of plus or minus 2. So between 5-9 separate pieces of information at a time.

While most psychologists still agree that 5-9 is accurate, more specifically they believe that it is not the number of items but instead how long it takes you to repeat that information to yourself. 

## Chunking

**Chunking** is the process of breaking information down into simple smaller easier to retrieve parts that you can then reconstruct into the whole. 

# The Working Memory Model

The **Working Memory Model** argues that your working memory functions as a place to manipulate information rather than a place to store, hence the name working memory.

In this model there is the **phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, and the central executive**

**The phonological loop** is where auditory information is stored and manipulated.

**The Visuospatial Sketchpad** is the inner eye, where you manipulate and store visual information.

**The Central Executive** is the process that decides what information stays and what gets thrown out.
