- While the physical body does have some restorative benefits from sleep, the most important purpose of sleep is really to rest the brain.

- Generally, the deeper asleep you are, the greater the amplitude of the brain waves will be. 

# Non REM Sleep

**Alpha Waves**: Observed when a person is awake but relaxed.

**Beta Waves**: Observed when a person is awake, alert, and actively processing information. Also appear during REM sleep.

**Sleep Spindles**: brief bursts of activity that occur roughly 2-5 times per minute during the non REM stages of sleep.
- The current thinking is that they play a role in memory consolidation, and a higher number of sleep spindles per night is associated with higher scores on standard IQ tests.

**K-Complexes**: Occur during stage 2 sleep, have relatively high amplitude. 
- They can be triggered by unexpected noises in the environment, but can also occur spontaneously. 

**Delta Waves**: Slow, regular, high amplitude waves. Slow-wave sleep is our deepest stage of sleep.

# REM Sleep

After about 45 minutes after the beginning of slow wave sleep, our brain waves change dramatically. As we transition from slow wave sleep into **REM** (**Rapid Eye Movement**) sleep, several physiological changes occur. 
- Rapid Eye Movement.
- Increased and Irregular Heart rate.
- Increase in blood pressure.
- Increase in breathing rate
- Erection in males (even babies)
- Usually accompanied by dreams.

Your brain during REM Sleep becomes very active. In fact the EEG of a brain during REM sleep looks more awake than it does asleep.

REM Sleep is much closer to the surface and easier to wake up from than a deeper level of sleep 

During this stage, blood flow in the brain is generally reduced, but the visual association cortex and the prefrontal cortex receive a large proportion of oxygenated blood.
- This is important because it provides the material for us to hallucinate our dreams and not act them out.

***NOTE:** REM Sleep Disorder involves the loss of paralysis during REM sleep. Individuals with this disorder actually can (and do) act out their dreams, which can be dangerous for those around them. ^88ea43

After REM, the brain returns to stage 1 sleep and the cycle repeats. Importantly:
1. As the night goes on, we spend less time in slow-wave sleep. This ensures that we get the deepest and most restorative sleep first, and also makes sure that we are more prepared to wake up during the later hours of sleep.
2. We spend more time in REM sleep as we go through the night. REM sleep is a shallow stage of sleep, so although it is incredibly important, it is also easier for your body to emerge from REM than from deeper levels of sleep. Thus, it makes sense for it to occur more often toward the later part of our sleep time.

# Why Do We Need Sleep?

After the brain is deprived of REM sleep, it begins to prioritize it by entering REM more quickly and spending proportionally more time there. This is called the **REM Rebound Effect**, which strongly suggests REM sleep is a biological necessity.
