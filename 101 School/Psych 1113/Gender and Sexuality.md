**Gender Expression** - The way a person communicates their gender identity to others by the way they dress, act, or refer to themselves. 

**Gender Identity** - A person's internal sense of their gender.

**Orientation/Attraction** - A term to describe sexual and/or romantic attractions to others.

**Sex** - The label male, female, or intersex given to someone at birth based on their body parts.

**Differences of Sexual Development (DSD)** - occurs in 1-2% of the population.

# Gender Similarity Hypothesis

- Largest analysis to date examined 106 meta-analyses; 85% showed little to no differences (Zell et al., 2015).

- Most gender differences are context-dependent.

- **Aggression:** behavior that is intended to harm another person.
	- **Overt Aggression:** direct physical or verbal harm
	- **Relational Aggression:** behavior meant to harm the social standing of another person
		- Gossip, spreading rumors, etc.

- Men engage in more physical aggression than women, but difference decreases with age.

- Relational aggression makes up a larger proportion of girls' total aggressive behavior.

# Gender Differences: Sexuality

- Men engage in more masturbation, view more pornography, engage in more casual sex, and have more permissive attitudes about casual sex than women.


# Biological Theories of Gender

- Evolutionary Theory: 
	- Gender differences maximize adaptation and natural selection.
	- Parental Investment Theory
		- Females invest more in offspring, choosier about mates, more nurturing.
		- Males invest less, compete for mates, more risk taking.
	- Examples:
		- Standards of beauty indicate fertility.
		- Spatial and verbal abilities.
		- Composite faces reflect genetic diversity.
	- Problems:
		- Difficult to test directly.
		- Doesn't explain cultural variation.

- Hormonal Influence Theory:
	- Hormones shape our development.
		- **Organizing effects:** Prenatal testosterone masculinizes the developing brain
			- Critical Periods during fetal development
		- **Activating effects:** Hormones "activate" organized pathways during puberty
			- Trigger gender-typical behaviors and interests.
	- Examples: 
		- Girls exposed to too much testosterone display more masculine behavior and vice-versa.
		- Hormone manipulation changes animal behavior.
		- Assigning a gender to **DSD** patients does not work
			- "Joan-John Case"
	- Problems:
		- Correlation != causation.
		- Environment **always** contributes.

# Social Learning Theory of Gender

- Focuses on how children learn gender roles through observation, imitation, and reinforcement
	- Emphasizes external models and consequences.
	- Emphasizes gender as a social construction.

- Strength: accounts for cultural differences.
- Weakness: Doesn't explain early gender preferences or animal behaviors.
